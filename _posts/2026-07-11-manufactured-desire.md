Last time I left a question hanging. The alienated analyst — comfortable office, slide decks that vanish into folders — is given a workshop. Evenings and weekends making things that are entirely her own. Does that *cure* her alienation, or merely *sedate* it?

I want to follow that question until it breaks, because where it breaks is more interesting than any answer I could give it.

## The obvious escape, and why it closes

Start with the tempting move: **let her quit.** She sells the furniture. She makes her living from the thing she loves. Surely that's liberation — she chose it, she controls it, she keeps the proceeds.

But attach the condition that makes it liberation, and watch what happens. She's free *only if she makes what she wants, rather than what sells.*

In a market, **what sells is what other people want.** So the condition amounts to: she is free as long as she can ignore what everyone else wants and still eat.

And now the trap. If nobody buys, she's back in the office by spring. If people *do* buy, then what she wanted and what sold have coincided — and here is the thing that should keep you up at night:

**From the outside, you cannot tell whether she made what she wanted, or gradually came to want what sells.**

Worse: *from the inside, neither can she.*

## The nastier possibility

The easy story about markets is that they *coerce* — they force your hand, and you resent it, and you know you're being forced. That story is manageable, because resentment is a kind of proof. You can feel the pressure, so you know it's there.

The harder story is that markets **quietly edit your desires** until you experience their demands as your own preferences.

She still feels free. She reports being fulfilled. She would tell you, sincerely, that she makes exactly what she loves. And every word of that is true — *and it is also true that a different market would have produced a different her, who would say the same sentence about different furniture.*

If your wants can be manufactured, then "but I wanted it" stops being proof of freedom. It becomes precisely the thing that needs explaining.

This is roughly where Adorno and Horkheimer set up camp, and it is why they are so exhausting to read: once you see it, the sincerity of a preference stops counting as evidence for anything.

## Three ways out, all damaged

So: **how would she know?** How would anyone know, from the inside, whether they want what they want, or want what they were made to want?

I can find three answers. None of them survives intact.

**One: there is no test, and demanding one is the error.** Preferences are simply what they are. There is no authentic want buried under a manufactured one, waiting to be dug up. If she says she is happy, on whose authority do you inform her that she is secretly deceived?

*The cost:* this makes manipulation almost impossible to criticise. Advertising, propaganda, the whole grim science of persuasion — all of it dissolves into neutral "preference formation." Something has obviously gone wrong, and we cannot say what.

**Two: the test is counterfactual.** Ask her: *would you still make this if it stopped selling?* If yes, the want is hers. If she would drift toward whatever moved, the market was steering.

*The cost:* she cannot run this on herself honestly — she will answer with whatever story flatters her, and she will believe it. And you can only run it on her by actually removing her income, which is not an experiment so much as a punishment.

**Three: the test is not in her head at all.** Stop asking what she wants. Ask what she can *afford* to want. Could she make an unsellable thing for six months without becoming destitute? If yes, her preferences are her own, whatever they happen to be. If no, then the question of what she wants never got a chance to be asked in the first place.

*The cost:* it gives up on the inner life entirely. It says: I cannot see inside you and neither can you, so let us stop looking there.

## Why the third one is the interesting one

Notice what Route Three does. It **relocates the entire problem** — out of psychology, into material conditions.

It stops trying to excavate an authentic self from under the conditioning. It asks instead: *what conditions does a self need in order to have wants at all?*

Freedom, on this account, is not an inner state you can introspect your way into. It is a set of circumstances under which wanting is even *possible* — where you could have chosen otherwise without starving.

And this, I think, is Marx's actual move. It is why he writes about factories and wages rather than feelings. Not because the inner life doesn't matter, but because he doesn't trust it as evidence. Ask a man whether he is free and he will tell you yes. Ask what happens to him if he stops working on Monday, and you will learn considerably more.

## Where this leaves the analyst

I don't think her workshop is a cure. But I no longer think it's *merely* a sedative either, and the reason is uncomfortable.

The workshop is what makes Monday survivable. Without it, she might quit, might organise, might revolt. With it, she can bear the office indefinitely. The hobby is not a refuge from the system — it may be **load-bearing infrastructure for it**, the thing that lets alienation continue by making it endurable.

Which is a bleak thought, and I'm not fully sure I believe it. It proves too much: by that logic, anything that makes a bad situation bearable is complicit in it, including friendship, and art, and sleep.

## The counterweight

I should say plainly what's wrong with all of this.

The manufactured-desire argument has a self-serving structure that ought to make you suspicious. It lets the theorist say: *you only think you're happy; I can see what you really want better than you can.* That is an extraordinary claim of authority, and historically the people who have made it have not covered themselves in glory.

There is also no obvious floor. If all desire is shaped by circumstance — and it is, all of it, always — then "manufactured" ceases to pick out anything in particular. Every want I have was produced by a history I did not choose. If that's alienation, then alienation is just *being a person*, and the word has stopped doing any work.

I don't know how to answer that. It might be fatal. It might not.

---

**A question I can't yet answer:** Route Three says freedom is the ability to want otherwise *without being destroyed for it* — the material room to have made a different choice. But nobody has unlimited room. Everyone's options are bounded by something: money, time, mortality, the century they were born into. So how much room is enough? Is there a threshold, or is this a difference of degree that we've been dressing up as a difference in kind?
